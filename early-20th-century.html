Early 20th Century

Women in Early 20th Century Medicine

The early 20th century saw women breaking barriers in medicine, education, and nursing, with women becoming more involved in scientific research and medical education.

"Nothing in life is to be feared; it is only to be understood."

Marie Curie became a pioneering physicist and chemist, best known for her discovery of radium and polonium, and her groundbreaking research on radioactivity.

"Apgar’s test is a system of measuring how well a newborn baby is doing, and it has saved many lives."

Virginia Apgar was a physician who developed the Apgar Score, a system for assessing the health of newborns, which became a standard in hospitals worldwide.

"Heart disease in children is still a relatively new concept, but we can cure it with surgery."

Helen Brooke Taussig was a physician and the founder of pediatric cardiology, who helped develop the first open-heart surgery for children with congenital heart defects.

Women’s pioneering work in the early 20th century in medicine and healthcare paved the way for greater rights and responsibilities in the healthcare field, in line with the NHD theme.
